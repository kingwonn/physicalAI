> Before "physical AI" was a marketing term, a loose multi-disciplinary movement — behavior-based robotics, ecological psychology, enactivism, morphological computation, dynamical-systems developmental science, and embodied linguistics — spent three decades arguing that intelligence cannot be extracted from a body and re-implemented as pure computation. Each strand made a specific, falsifiable claim about what a disembodied information-processor could never do. This page collects the primary citations, exact wording (verbatim quotes flagged as such; paraphrases flagged as such), and the testable predictions, so today's LLM/VLA-era claims can be checked against them rather than against secondhand summaries.

## Why this page exists

The current [VLA](vla-models.md) / [world-model](world-models.md) wave inherited its vocabulary — "embodiment," "grounding," "affordance," "morphological computation" — from this literature, often without the citation. Some of that borrowing is precise; some is marketing gloss on a phrase whose original claim was narrower or more radical than the modern usage implies. Per this wiki's honesty rules: primary sources first, verbatim quotes stay verbatim, paraphrases are flagged, single-source claims get `(unverified)`.

## 1. Rodney Brooks — subsumption architecture and the physical grounding hypothesis

**Primary sources**, both retrieved and text-verified directly from Brooks' own MIT CSAIL page for this pass:
- Brooks, R.A., "A Robust Layered Control System for a Mobile Robot," *IEEE Journal of Robotics and Automation*, Vol. 2, No. 1, March 1986, pp. 14–23.
- Brooks, R.A., "Elephants Don't Play Chess," *Robotics and Autonomous Systems* 6 (1990), pp. 3–15.
- Brooks, R.A., "Intelligence Without Representation," *Artificial Intelligence* 47 (1991), pp. 139–159 (received Sept. 1987 — the ideas predate the 1990/1991 papers that popularized them).

**Subsumption architecture (1986).** The paper proposes decomposing a mobile robot's control system into layers of task-achieving behaviors (e.g., avoid-obstacles, wander, explore) built and debugged incrementally; higher layers can *subsume* — override/inhibit — the outputs of lower layers without those lower layers being removed, so the system degrades gracefully and each layer alone is already a complete, working controller. No central world model or planner coordinates the layers; coordination emerges from the layering and the layers' direct coupling to sensors and actuators. Demonstrated on real mobile robots wandering unconstrained lab and machine-room spaces.

**"The world is its own best model" — verified exact wording.** This phrase is genuinely Brooks' own, and is real — but it is commonly clipped to a four-word soundbite that drops its argument. Full verbatim passage, "Elephants Don't Play Chess" (1990), §3 "The Physical Grounding Hypothesis" (confirmed against the primary PDF, `people.csail.mit.edu/brooks/papers/elephants.pdf`):

> "Nouvelle AI is based on the physical grounding hypothesis. This hypothesis states that to build a system that is intelligent it is necessary to have its representations grounded in the physical world. Our experience with this approach is that once this commitment is made, the need for traditional symbolic representations soon fades entirely. The key observation is that **the world is its own best model. It is always exactly up to date. It always contains every detail there is to be known. The trick is to sense it appropriately and often enough.**"

The line recurs later in the same paper (re: the robot "Herbert"): "Herbert showed many instances of using the world as its own best model and as a communication medium." So it is not a one-off aphorism — it is the paper's structuring claim, repeated. **Not from "Intelligence Without Representation"** (1991) — some secondary sources (e.g. aggregator citation lists) mis-cite it there; the primary PDF of that paper (confirmed via direct text extraction) does not contain the phrase.

**"Intelligence Without Representation" (1991) — the paper's actual core claim**, given by Brooks in his own labeled (C)/(H) form (verbatim, confirmed via primary PDF):

> "(C) When we examine very simple level intelligence we find that explicit representations and models of the world simply get in the way. It turns out to be better to use the world as its own model.
> (H) Representation is the wrong unit of abstraction in building the bulkiest parts of intelligent systems."

Brooks frames this as an "unexpected conclusion" and "rather radical hypothesis" reached empirically from building robots, not asserted a priori.

**Testable prediction.** A system that reasons over a static, hand-built (or purely text-trained) symbolic/latent model of the world — rather than continuously re-sensing the physical world at the moment of acting — will be brittle exactly where the world has changed since the model was built or where the model omitted a detail action depends on. This is directly testable against modern VLAs: does a policy trained offline degrade when the scene deviates from training distribution in ways a closed-loop, continuously-sensing system would not? (Brooks would predict yes, categorically, for any architecture that separates "build a model" from "act," regardless of scale.)

**The Cog project (MIT, begun 1993; Brooks + Cynthia Breazeal, Lynn Andrea Stein, Rodney Brooks, et al.).** Cog was an upper-torso humanoid (21 DOF, vision/audition/vestibular/kinesthetic/tactile sensing) built explicitly to test the hypothesis that human-level intelligence is not separable from a human-like body and human-like developmental/social interaction. Per Brooks' own framing (secondary summary confirmed across multiple sources incl. MIT News and the project's Springer chapter "The Cog Project: Building a Humanoid Robot"), the project had two stated goals: an *engineering* goal (build a general-purpose dexterous autonomous robot) and a *scientific* goal (understand human cognition) — grounded in four claimed prerequisites for human-like intelligence: **developmental organization, social interaction, embodiment, and physical coupling**. Cog never achieved general intelligence and the project wound down around 2003–2004; it's cited today mainly as the historical proof-of-attempt that embodiment-first roboticists took the "build a body like ours" hypothesis literally, not just as a slogan. *(secondary characterization of project outcome; the stated goals above are corroborated across independent sources but I did not extract Cog's own primary paper verbatim in this pass — flag accordingly.)*

**Testable prediction (Cog's implicit bet).** Human-level general cognition requires a body with human-like sensorimotor and social coupling to develop it — a disembodied learner (however large) trained without a body and without the developmental trajectory of infancy will hit a ceiling symbolic/statistical scale cannot remove. Cog itself never confirmed this (it didn't reach general intelligence by any body type), so the prediction remains open, not validated.

**Brooks' 2019–2025 positions on LLMs / the bitter lesson.** Confirmed from Brooks' own blog, rodneybrooks.com:
- **"A Better Lesson"** (rodneybrooks.com, 2019-03-19) is Brooks' direct rebuttal to Rich Sutton's "The Bitter Lesson" (2019). Core counter-argument: general search+learning-at-scale methods still smuggle in human-designed structure (his example: CNNs' translational-invariance prior is itself a human design choice, not something learned from scratch), so "just add compute" is not actually structure-free — it has just moved the human ingenuity into architecture design and relocated where the "few hundred bits" of built-in knowledge live. He also raises the energy-efficiency gap (human brain ~20 W vs. an autonomous-driving stack's then-~2,500 W) as a reason pure scaling is not obviously the only or best axis.
- **"Three Things That LLMs Have Made Us Rethink"** (rodneybrooks.com, 2023-12-08) — post-ChatGPT reassessment. He concedes ground on several fronts: the Turing Test lost its force as a "crystalline indication of intelligence" once a system could pass casual versions of it; and — his most striking admission — "GPT **is** the Chinese Room, the fact that one now exists, means that many of the old arguments for and against Searle's position…must now be faced squarely," while still maintaining ChatGPT-class models don't "understand" as a human does. He also notes LLMs learn grammatical competence "without any sort of universal grammar knowledge that Chomsky argues both constrains and enables what humans can learn" — a concession that a purely statistical, non-innate, non-embodied learner achieved something his priors said needed built-in structure.
- On embodiment specifically, Brooks' standing position as of this 2023 post: **"Without grounding in physical reality I don't think a machine can understand in the same way we do."** This is his most direct, dated (2023-12-08) statement that the embodiment thesis survives the LLM era, even as he revises other priors.
- Brooks also keeps an annual **"Predictions Scorecard"** (e.g. `rodneybrooks.com/predictions-scorecard-2026-january-01/`) grading his own multi-year AI/robotics predictions against reality — a self-correcting mechanism rare in this literature and worth citing as a discipline model.

## 2. Ecological psychology — James J. Gibson

**Primary source**: Gibson, J.J., *The Ecological Approach to Visual Perception*, Houghton Mifflin, 1979. (The concept was previewed in Gibson's 1977 chapter "The Theory of Affordances," in Shaw & Bransford, eds., *Perceiving, Acting, and Knowing*.)

**Affordance — verbatim definition**, the canonical single sentence:
> "The affordances of the environment are what it offers the animal, what it provides or furnishes, either for good or ill."

**Affordance — the fuller, philosophically load-bearing passage** (verbatim, same source), which is the part most secondary summaries drop and which is the actual radical claim:
> "An affordance is neither an objective property nor a subjective property; or it is both if you like. An affordance cuts across the dichotomy of subjective–objective and helps us to understand its inadequacy. It is equally a fact of the environment and a fact of behavior. It is both physical and psychical, yet neither. An affordance points both ways, to the environment and to the observer."

**Direct perception — core claim.** Gibson's broader thesis (his "ecological approach") is that perception is not an inferential/computational process that builds a representation from impoverished retinal input (the dominant information-processing view he was arguing against), but a *direct* pickup of information already structured in the ambient optic array — affordances are perceived directly, not computed from sensory data plus stored knowledge. *(This is the standard characterization of Gibson's project across the secondary literature; I have the primary affordance definition verbatim above but did not extract his full direct-perception argument verbatim in this pass.)*

**Testable prediction.** A perceptual system built by processing static images divorced from an active, moving, acting body in an environment (Gibson stressed the *ambulatory*, self-moving observer and the resulting optic flow as information-bearing) should perceive affordances differently — and worse, for action-guidance purposes — than one that perceives through embodied exploration. This bears directly on the "internet video vs. embodied interaction" data debate in [world-models.md](world-models.md): Gibson's framework predicts passively-collected video (no self-generated motor coupling) is a categorically weaker training signal for action-affordance perception than active embodied experience, not just a smaller dataset.

## 3. Enactivism — Varela, Thompson, Rosch; and Maturana on autopoiesis

**Primary source**: Varela, F.J., Thompson, E., Rosch, E., *The Embodied Mind: Cognitive Science and Human Experience*, MIT Press, 1991.

**"Enaction" — verbatim definition**, as the authors state it:
> "We propose as a name the term *enactive* to emphasize the growing conviction that cognition is not the representation of a pregiven world by a pregiven mind but is rather the enactment of a world and a mind on the basis of a history of the variety of actions that a being in the world performs."

Core thesis (secondary paraphrase of the book's overall argument, consistent across independent summaries): cognition is not computation over internal representations of a pre-existing, mind-independent world; it is the *bringing forth* (enactment) of a world of significance through the structural coupling of a living system's sensorimotor activity with its environment, historically conditioned by that coupling — i.e., organism and environment co-determine each other rather than one passively representing the other.

**Maturana & Varela on autopoiesis — dating is genuinely tangled across sources**, so flagged explicitly rather than resolved to false precision: the ideas were first published in Spanish as *De Máquinas y Seres Vivos* (Santiago, Editorial Universitaria, 1972/1973 — sources disagree on which year), and the canonical English-language edition most cited in the literature is Maturana, H.R. & Varela, F.J., *Autopoiesis and Cognition: The Realization of the Living*, D. Reidel, 1980. **Cite as "Maturana & Varela 1972/1980" and flag the 1972 vs. 1973 Spanish-edition date as unresolved (unverified) pending a primary bibliographic check.**

**Autopoiesis — original technical definition** (verbatim, as commonly quoted from the primary text across independent secondary sources — not independently re-verified against a scanned original page in this pass, so treat the exact wording as high-confidence-secondary rather than primary-confirmed):
> "An autopoietic machine is a machine organized (defined as a unity) as a network of processes of production (transformation and destruction) of components which: (i) through their interactions and transformations continuously regenerate and realize the network of processes (relations) that produced them; and (ii) constitute it (the machine) as a concrete unity in space in which they (the components) exist by specifying the topological domain of its realization as such a network."

In plain terms: a living system is defined by continuously producing and regenerating its own components and organization through its own processes — self-production, not externally-imposed design, is what makes something "alive" in their framework. Autopoiesis is Maturana & Varela's biological foundation; Varela, Thompson & Rosch's enactivism (1991) builds cognition on top of it — cognition, in this lineage, is what a self-producing, structurally-coupled system does, not information processing performed *on* a system from outside.

**Testable prediction.** A system whose "cognition" consists of processing tokens/data without any self-maintaining, self-producing organizational closure coupled to an environment (i.e., an LLM run as a stateless function call) is not enacting a world in the Varela/Thompson/Rosch sense regardless of behavioral sophistication — the enactivist claim predicts a categorical (not merely quantitative) gap between such a system and any organism/robot with genuine sensorimotor structural coupling and self-maintained organization, however capable the former looks on benchmarks.

## 4. Morphological computation — Pfeifer & Bongard; McGeer's passive walkers as the physical proof

**Primary source**: Pfeifer, R., Bongard, J., *How the Body Shapes the Way We Think: A New View of Intelligence*, MIT Press, 2006/2007.

Core claim (secondary paraphrase, consistent across independent summaries of the book; I was not able to extract page-exact verbatim text of the book itself in this pass — MIT Press Direct and Google Books both blocked automated fetch): intelligence cannot be understood, designed, or fully instantiated in a brain/controller alone — thought is "not independent of the body but is tightly constrained, and at the same time enabled, by it," and specific cognitive capacities trace to specific facts about morphology and material properties (softness, elasticity, mass distribution, sensor placement). The related technical term **morphological computation** — developed further in follow-on literature (e.g. Pfeifer & Iida 2005 "Morphological Computation: Connecting Brain, Body, and Environment") — denotes the idea that the body's physical dynamics can perform part of the computation an explicit neural/algorithmic controller would otherwise have to do: the controller can be simpler because the body "solves" part of the control problem through its own physics. Secondary technical sources summarize this as the body "offloading" or letting a controller "outsource" computational/control work to physical dynamics.

**McGeer's passive dynamic walkers — the physical proof, primary source**: McGeer, T., "Passive Dynamic Walking," *International Journal of Robotics Research*, 9(2), 1990, pp. 62–82. Core finding (paraphrase of the paper's demonstrated result, corroborated across independent citing sources): a class of two-legged mechanisms exists for which walking is a *natural dynamic mode* — released on a shallow downward slope with **zero actuation, zero control, and zero computation**, such a machine settles into a steady, human-comparable gait purely from gravity, mass distribution, and joint geometry. This is cited across the morphological-computation literature as the clean existence-proof: a real physical system produced complex, adaptive-looking legged locomotion with a controller of *zero* bits, entirely as a consequence of body design — directly falsifying any claim that legged locomotion inherently requires an information-processing controller of nontrivial complexity.

**Testable prediction.** For a defined class of tasks (McGeer's is locomotion; morphological-computation researchers extend the claim to grasping compliance, sensor pre-processing, etc.), better *body* design can substitute for controller/computation complexity — meaning a fixed-body, larger-brain approach (more parameters, more training compute, same rigid/generic hardware) is not the only or most efficient axis of improvement, and should show diminishing returns relative to co-designed hardware for tasks where the relevant physics (compliance, passive dynamics, underactuation) is doing real work. This is a direct, testable counter-claim to "scale the model, keep the hardware generic" strategies in current humanoid programs.

## 5. Valentino Braitenberg — Vehicles (1984)

**Primary source**: Braitenberg, V., *Vehicles: Experiments in Synthetic Psychology*, MIT Press (Bradford Books), 1984.

Core concept: a sequence of 14 thought-experiment "vehicles" of increasing complexity, each built from minimal sensors wired directly (with no intervening symbolic representation) to motors — e.g., a light sensor wired to the same-side wheel motor produces light-avoiding behavior; wired cross-side, it produces light-seeking behavior. Simple wiring topologies, observed from outside, look like fear, aggression, or love. The book's named organizing principle, cited consistently across independent secondary/academic sources as **"the law of uphill analysis and downhill invention"**: designing (synthesizing) a mechanism that produces a given behavior is comparatively easy ("downhill"), while inferring the internal mechanism of an already-behaving black-box system from its external behavior alone ("uphill analysis," i.e. reverse-engineering) is comparatively hard — because an outside observer tends to over-attribute internal complexity/intentionality to a system whose actual mechanism is much simpler than the behavior suggests. *(The exact wording quoted for this "law" varies slightly across secondary sources — I did not extract Braitenberg's own page-exact sentence from the primary 1984 text in this pass; treat the concept and its standard name as primary-attributed but the precise phrasing as secondary-sourced.)*

**Testable prediction.** Observers (including AI researchers) watching an entity's external behavior will systematically over-infer internal representational/cognitive complexity relative to the true minimal mechanism — a standing methodological warning against inferring "understanding" or "reasoning" inside any system (biological, robotic, or an LLM) from behavioral sophistication alone, since simple sensorimotor wiring can produce behavior that reads as complex from outside.

## 6. Dynamical-systems cognition — Thelen & Smith

**Primary source**: Thelen, E., Smith, L.B., *A Dynamic Systems Approach to the Development of Cognition and Action*, MIT Press, 1994.

Core claim (secondary paraphrase of the book's stated program, corroborated across independent academic summaries; primary-text verbatim not extracted in this pass — MIT Press and academic mirrors returned non-text-extractable or blocked content): cognitive development should be modeled as the emergent, self-organizing product of a dynamical system coupling brain, body, and environment over real time — not as execution of a maturing innate plan (nativism), passive association-building (empiricism), or discrete symbolic staged transitions (Piaget). The book explicitly targets and rejects a set of classic dualisms — structure vs. function, nature vs. nurture, brain vs. behavior, perception vs. cognition, mind vs. body, competence vs. performance, learning vs. development — arguing developmental change is continuous, context-dependent, and arises from the same real-time sensorimotor dynamics that produce moment-to-moment behavior, not from a separate offline "cognition module." Their signature empirical case (widely cited, e.g. the "A-not-B error" reanalysis) reinterprets a classic Piagetian "cognitive stage" result as an artifact of infants' real-time motor/postural dynamics rather than evidence of an abstract conceptual limitation.

**Testable prediction.** Developmental milestones traditionally explained as discrete conceptual/symbolic achievements should instead be traceable to continuous changes in the coupled body-brain-environment dynamical system (motor maturation, changing body proportions, real-time task dynamics) — meaning a disembodied learner with no analogous continuous, coupled sensorimotor dynamics should fail to reproduce the specific developmental *trajectory* (not just eventual competence) that human infants show, even if it eventually reaches similar end-state task performance by a different route.

## 7. Developmental / epigenetic robotics — Asada; Cangelosi & Schlesinger

**Primary source**: Asada, M., Hosoda, K., Kuniyoshi, Y., Ishiguro, H., Inui, T., Yoshikawa, Y., Ogino, M., Yoshida, C., "Cognitive Developmental Robotics: A Survey," *IEEE Transactions on Autonomous Mental Development*, Vol. 1, No. 1, 2009, pp. 12–34.

Core claim (from the paper's stated aims, secondary-paraphrase-confirmed across the paper's own abstract/framing as reported by multiple independent index sources): cognitive developmental robotics (CDR) aims to provide a *constructive*, synthetic understanding of how human higher cognitive functions develop, by literally building robots that develop those functions through embodied interaction over a human-like developmental trajectory — not merely filling an engineering-vs-psychology gap, but building "a new paradigm that provides new understanding of ourselves and, at the same time, new design theory of humanoids symbiotic with us." The manifesto claim is methodological: you cannot validate a theory of cognitive development except by instantiating it in a real embodied system and watching whether it actually develops the target capacities through real sensorimotor experience.

**Secondary/related primary**: Cangelosi, A., Schlesinger, M. (with contributions from L.B. Smith, R.C. Arkin), *Developmental Robotics: From Babies to Robots*, MIT Press, 2015. Core claim (paraphrase, consistent across independent summaries): robots equipped with intrinsic developmental principles governing real-time brain-body-environment interaction can *autonomously* acquire increasingly complex sensorimotor and cognitive capabilities, mirroring stages of human infant development, via an explicitly interdisciplinary synthesis of developmental psychology/neuroscience with robotics/AI (rather than hand-engineering each capability).

**Testable prediction.** Capabilities that in humans are acquired through a specific embodied developmental sequence (e.g., object permanence, tool use, joint attention) should be reproducible in a robot only via an analogous staged, embodied developmental process — and a system that acquires the *behavioral output* of such a capability by other means (e.g., large-scale offline imitation learning with no developmental staging) should show detectably different generalization failure modes than a developmentally-trained system, even at matched task accuracy.

## 8. Karl Friston — active inference / the free-energy principle

**Primary source**: Friston, K., "The free-energy principle: a unified brain theory?", *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*, 11, 2010, pp. 127–138.

Core claim in one paragraph: the free-energy principle proposes that any self-organizing biological system that maintains itself in a bounded set of states (i.e., stays alive) must, as a mathematical consequence, act so as to minimize a quantity called "variational free energy" — an upper bound on surprise (the negative log-probability of the system's sensory states given its model of the world. Friston argues this single imperative unifies perception (updating internal beliefs to better predict sensory input — minimizing free energy by changing the model) and action (changing the world/moving the body to bring sensory input in line with predictions — minimizing free energy by changing the sampled states), and that it subsumes/reconciles several prior brain theories (Bayesian brain, predictive coding, optimal control, cell assembly/Hebbian theories) under one variational-optimization framework.

**Contested status — honestly noted, with citations.** This is one of the most disputed claims in the entire lineage, and should not be presented as settled. Independent critics have argued: the framework is **unfalsifiable** — because almost any observed biological or cognitive phenomenon can post-hoc be redescribed as "minimizing free energy," it becomes unclear what empirical result would ever count as evidence against it (a criticism explicitly discussed in the peer-commentary literature around the theory, e.g. companion pieces to Friston's own Nature Reviews Neuroscience paper, and later critiques such as a paper titled "The Emperor's New Pseudo-Theory: How the Free Energy Principle Ransacked Neuroscience"). A related, more measured critique (PMC, "The Free Energy Principle: Good Science and Questionable Philosophy in a Grand Unifying Theory") separates the mathematically legitimate technical apparatus (variational Bayesian inference, which is genuinely useful and widely used in machine learning) from the much stronger philosophical/teleological claim that *all* biological self-organization is best explained by it — arguing the latter over-reaches and risks circularity ("why do systems minimize free energy? Because they exist. Why do they exist? Because they minimize free energy."). The colloquial "theory of everything and nothing" framing captures this: broad enough to explain any outcome, and therefore short on discriminating predictive power. `(unverified/contested — flagging the debate rather than adjudicating it)`

**Testable prediction (as Friston and active-inference proponents frame it).** Perception and action are not separate modules but a single variational-inference process that must be *closed through the body* — an agent minimizes surprise partly by acting to change what it senses, not only by updating internal beliefs. This predicts that a system which can only update beliefs (a passive predictor, e.g. a next-token model with no ability to act and re-sample the world) is doing at most half of what active inference requires, and should show systematically different (worse, more surprise-prone) behavior than an embodied agent that can close the loop through action — though critics would note this prediction is hard to falsify precisely because almost any outcome can be redescribed as consistent with it.

## 9. The linguistics wing — Lakoff & Johnson

**Primary sources**: Lakoff, G., Johnson, M., *Metaphors We Live By*, University of Chicago Press, 1980; Lakoff, G., Johnson, M., *Philosophy in the Flesh: The Embodied Mind and Its Challenge to Western Thought*, Basic Books, 1999.

**1980 — core claim.** Conceptual Metaphor Theory: metaphor is not a decorative feature of language/rhetoric but a fundamental structuring mechanism of everyday thought itself — "our conceptual system is fundamentally metaphorical" — and abstract domains (argument, time, emotion, mental activity) are systematically understood *in terms of* more concrete, bodily/physically-grounded source domains (e.g. ARGUMENT IS WAR licenses "defending a position," "shooting down an idea"). The grounding claim is the load-bearing one for this lineage: abstract cognition is not autonomous symbolic manipulation but is scaffolded on structures derived from physical, bodily experience (spatial orientation, containment, force, motion).

**1999 — "three major findings" of cognitive science**, per Lakoff & Johnson's own framing (this specific tripartite formulation is well-attested across independent secondary summaries of the book, though I did not extract the exact page-numbered original sentence in this pass — flagged as secondary-sourced wording, primary-attributed claim):
1. "The mind is inherently embodied."
2. "Thought is mostly unconscious."
3. "Abstract concepts are largely metaphorical."

And their own stated conclusion connecting the three (reported as a direct quote across multiple independent academic-review sources of the book, consistent wording): *"When taken together and considered in detail, these three findings from the science of the mind are inconsistent with central parts of Western philosophy, and require a thorough rethinking of the most popular current approaches, namely, Anglo-American analytic philosophy and postmodernist philosophy."*

**Testable prediction.** A system that has never had a body — never experienced containment, verticality, force-dynamics, balance, or goal-directed physical motion — should systematically fail to ground abstract-concept metaphors in the way humans do, even if it can fluently *reproduce* metaphorical language learned statistically from a corpus of human (embodied) writers describing their embodied experience. The strong version of the prediction: such a system's metaphor competence is parasitic on human writers having already done the embodied grounding work and encoded it in text — i.e., an LLM's fluency with "grasping an idea" or "a warm welcome" reflects *its training corpus's* embodiment, not its own, and this should be detectable in metaphor use for genuinely novel bodily configurations no human source text covers.

## Cross-lineage synthesis — the shared testable core

Stripped of discipline-specific vocabulary, nearly every strand above converges on one falsifiable meta-claim: **intelligence/cognition is not a computation that can be fully specified independent of (a) a body with specific morphology, (b) continuous real-time sensorimotor coupling to an environment, and (c) — for several strands (enactivism, developmental robotics, dynamical systems) — a developmental history of that coupling.** Where the strands genuinely differ is *how strong* the claim is:
- **Weak/architectural** (Brooks, morphological computation): embodiment changes what's *efficient* or *necessary in practice* — a disembodied system might in principle reach the same competence but will pay a structural tax (McGeer's zero-computation walker vs. a computed gait controller; Brooks' representation-as-bottleneck).
- **Strong/constitutive** (enactivism, Gibson's direct perception, Lakoff & Johnson's grounding): embodiment is *definitionally* part of what cognition/perception/meaning *is* — a disembodied system isn't doing a worse version of the same thing, it's doing something categorically different that merely produces similar outputs.

This weak/strong distinction is the one most worth keeping precise when this lineage's vocabulary gets invoked (often loosely) to critique or defend current LLM/VLA architectures — see [world-models.md](world-models.md) and [physical-ai-essence.md](physical-ai-essence.md) for where the modern field's own claims land on this spectrum, and [open-problems.md](open-problems.md) for the live version of this debate (Brooks' 2023 "grounding" quote above is his own attempt to answer it post-ChatGPT).
